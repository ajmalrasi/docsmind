# Black Holes

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing —
not even light — can escape once it crosses the boundary. They form when matter
is compressed into a small enough volume that its escape velocity exceeds the
speed of light.

## The event horizon

The event horizon is the boundary of a black hole, not a physical surface. It is
the point of no return: anything that crosses it, including light, can never get
back out. The radius of the event horizon for a non-rotating black hole is called
the Schwarzschild radius, and it grows in proportion to the black hole's mass. An
outside observer never actually sees an object cross the horizon — it appears to
slow and fade as time dilation stretches its light toward the red.

## The singularity

At the very center lies the singularity, a point where matter is crushed to
infinite density and the known laws of physics break down. Everything that falls
past the event horizon is drawn inevitably toward the singularity.

## How black holes form

Stellar-mass black holes form when a massive star — typically more than about 20
times the mass of the Sun — exhausts its fuel and collapses under its own gravity
in a supernova. Supermassive black holes, millions to billions of times the mass
of the Sun, sit at the centers of most galaxies; the one at the heart of the
Milky Way is called Sagittarius A*.

## Accretion disks and Hawking radiation

Matter spiraling toward a black hole forms a superheated accretion disk that
emits intense X-rays before crossing the horizon — this is often how black holes
are detected. In theory, black holes are not entirely black: they slowly emit
Hawking radiation and, over immense timescales, can evaporate entirely.
