# Rocket Propulsion

A rocket moves by throwing mass in one direction to push itself in the other.
This is a direct consequence of Newton's third law: for every action there is an
equal and opposite reaction. By expelling hot gas backward at high speed, the
rocket is pushed forward.

## Thrust

Thrust is the forward force produced by the engine. It depends on how much mass
the engine expels per second and how fast that mass leaves the nozzle. Crucially,
a rocket carries its own propellant and oxidizer, so unlike a jet engine it does
not need outside air — which is why rockets work in the vacuum of space.

## Specific impulse

Specific impulse measures how efficiently a rocket uses propellant — essentially
the thrust produced per unit of propellant burned per second. A higher specific
impulse means more push from the same amount of fuel. It is the key number
engineers use to compare engine designs.

## Chemical propellants

Most launch vehicles use chemical rockets. Liquid-fuel engines, such as those
burning liquid hydrogen with liquid oxygen, can be throttled and restarted and
are highly efficient. Solid-fuel rockets are simpler and provide enormous thrust
but cannot be shut off once ignited.

## Staging

Carrying empty fuel tanks wastes thrust, so large rockets are built in stages.
When a lower stage burns out, it is jettisoned, shedding dead weight so the
remaining engines accelerate a much lighter vehicle. This staging is what makes
reaching orbit possible.

## Escape velocity

To leave Earth's gravity entirely, a spacecraft must reach escape velocity, about
11.2 kilometers per second. Reaching orbit requires building up this enormous
speed, which is why launch vehicles are mostly propellant by mass.
