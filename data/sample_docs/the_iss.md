# The International Space Station

The International Space Station (ISS) is a large, crewed laboratory orbiting
Earth. It is the result of a partnership among the space agencies of the United
States, Russia, Europe, Japan, and Canada, and has been continuously occupied by
rotating crews since November 2000.

## Orbit

The ISS orbits in Low Earth Orbit at an altitude of roughly 400 kilometers. It
travels at about 28,000 kilometers per hour, completing one orbit of Earth
approximately every 90 minutes. That means the crew sees about 16 sunrises and
sunsets every day. Atmospheric drag slowly lowers the station, so it is
periodically boosted back to a higher altitude.

## Microgravity

Astronauts aboard the station appear weightless, but this is not because gravity
is absent — gravity at that altitude is nearly as strong as on the surface.
Instead, the station and everyone in it are in continuous free fall around the
planet, moving sideways fast enough that they keep missing the Earth. This state
is called microgravity, and it is the main reason the station exists: it lets
scientists run experiments impossible to perform on the ground.

## Structure

The station is built from pressurized modules connected together, with large
solar arrays providing electrical power and radiators shedding waste heat. Crews
typically number around seven and live aboard for months at a time, conducting
research in biology, physics, and materials science, and studying how the human
body adapts to long-duration spaceflight.
