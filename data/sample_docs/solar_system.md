# The Solar System

The Solar System is the Sun and everything gravitationally bound to it: eight
planets, their moons, dwarf planets, asteroids, and comets. The Sun contains
about 99.86% of the system's total mass, so its gravity governs the orbits of
everything else.

## The Sun

The Sun is a main-sequence star that fuses hydrogen into helium in its core,
releasing the energy that lights and warms the planets. It is the gravitational
anchor of the entire system.

## Terrestrial planets

The four inner planets — Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars — are small, rocky
worlds with solid surfaces. They formed close to the Sun where only metals and
rock could condense from the early solar nebula.

## Gas and ice giants

Beyond Mars lie the giants. Jupiter and Saturn are gas giants, enormous balls of
hydrogen and helium with no solid surface. Uranus and Neptune are ice giants,
containing more water, ammonia, and methane. All four are far more massive than
the terrestrial planets and hold large systems of moons and rings.

## Small bodies

Between Mars and Jupiter lies the asteroid belt, a band of rocky debris left over
from the system's formation. Beyond Neptune is the Kuiper Belt, a vast region of
icy bodies that includes the dwarf planet Pluto. Comets, which originate in these
cold outer regions, grow bright tails when their ices vaporize on approach to the
Sun.

## Dwarf planets

Dwarf planets such as Pluto, Eris, and Ceres are massive enough to be rounded by
their own gravity but have not cleared their orbital paths of other debris — the
distinction that separates them from the eight planets.
