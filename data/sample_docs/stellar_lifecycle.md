# The Life Cycle of a Star

Stars are born, live for millions to billions of years, and die. How a star ends
its life depends almost entirely on one thing: its mass. Low-mass stars fade
quietly, while massive stars end in violent explosions.

## Birth in a nebula

Stars form inside nebulae — vast clouds of gas and dust. When a dense region of
the cloud collapses under its own gravity, it heats up and forms a protostar.
Once the core becomes hot and dense enough, hydrogen fusion ignites and a true
star is born.

## The main sequence

For most of its life a star is on the main sequence, steadily fusing hydrogen
into helium in its core. The outward pressure from fusion balances the inward
pull of gravity, keeping the star stable. Our Sun is a main-sequence star and
will remain in this phase for about ten billion years in total.

## The death of low-mass stars

When a star like the Sun exhausts its core hydrogen, it swells into a red giant.
Eventually it sheds its outer layers as a glowing planetary nebula, leaving
behind a hot, dense core called a white dwarf, which slowly cools over billions
of years.

## The death of massive stars

A star much more massive than the Sun burns through its fuel quickly and becomes
a red supergiant. When its core can no longer support itself, it collapses and
rebounds in a supernova — one of the most energetic events in the universe. What
remains depends on mass: a moderately massive core forms a neutron star, while
the most massive cores collapse completely into a black hole.
